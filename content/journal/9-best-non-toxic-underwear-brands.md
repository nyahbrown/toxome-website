faq:
  - q: "Is polyester underwear bad for you?"
    a: "Polyester is the fiber with the least reassuring evidence behind it and, in this category, the most contact time against the thinnest skin on the body. It also traps heat and moisture in a way cotton does not, which is why the standard clinical advice for recurring irritation starts with switching to breathable natural fiber. Reducing what sits there for sixteen hours a day is the claim worth making, and it is the only one worth making."
  - q: "Why does the gusset matter more than the rest of the pair?"
    a: "Because it is the panel in direct contact with mucosal tissue, which absorbs more readily than the skin on your arm. Plenty of synthetic underwear is sold with a cotton gusset for exactly this reason, and that is a genuine improvement over an all-polyester pair. It is still a patch on the problem rather than a solution, since the rest of the garment sits against skin too."
  - q: "Is a few percent of elastane a problem?"
    a: "No, and any pair with a stretch fit contains some. The bulk of the fabric is what matters. A pair at 95% organic cotton and 5% elastane is a cotton pair. A pair at 60% nylon is not, whatever the packaging says about breathability."
  - q: "Does the waistband count?"
    a: "It is the part brands most often leave out of the composition line, and the part that presses hardest into skin all day. Most stretch waistbands are synthetic. A few brands do it differently, using natural rubber or covered cotton elastic, and they tend to say so on the product page. If a waistband is not described anywhere, assume it is synthetic."
  - q: "Is organic cotton different from regular cotton here?"
    a: "For a garment this close to the body, it is the swap to make first. A full GOTS certificate covers the dye house and not only the field, and the dye house is where most of the chemistry that reaches your skin gets added. Conventional cotton can be good fiber that then goes through processing nobody discloses."
  - q: "How often should underwear be replaced?"
    a: "Elastic fatigues long before the fabric does, so a pair that has gone slack at the waistband is done regardless of how the cotton looks. Buying fewer, better pairs and rotating them is easier on the garment than owning a large drawer you wash on high heat."

Underwear is the one category where the argument makes itself. Every other garment gets taken off, layered over, worn loose. Underwear sits against the most permeable skin on the body for sixteen hours a day, through heat and friction, and then gets replaced with another pair for the other eight.

That makes contact time the whole game, and the drawer is where it goes wrong. The five-for-$25 multipack is nylon and elastane. So is most of what gets sold as breathable, moisture-wicking or seamless, because those three words all describe things synthetic knits do well. The category has spent two decades optimizing for a smooth line under leggings, and polyester is very good at a smooth line.

The fix is not complicated, which is the frustrating part. Cotton underwear was the default within living memory. It still exists, it still costs less than the technical version at the same brands, and the brands making it well are easy to find once you know that the composition line matters more than the marketing above it.

So here are nine pairs made of fiber worth sitting in, from $8 to $55:

The top of this list is single-fiber and undyed, the middle is hemp, and the bottom is 95% cotton with a little stretch. All of it beats the multipack, and the cheapest pair here costs $8.
