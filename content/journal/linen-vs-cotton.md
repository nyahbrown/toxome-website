This is the rare comparison where there's no villain. Linen and cotton are both natural plant fibers, both breathable, both free of the plastic that hides in most of the clothing rack. Neither will quietly shed microplastics into your laundry water, and neither was dissolved in harsh chemistry to exist. So this isn't a question of safe versus not. It's a question of which good option is right for the piece in your hand, and the answer changes depending on what you're buying it for.

They come from different plants, and that's where every difference begins. Cotton is the soft fluff that grows around the seed of the cotton plant. Linen is spun from the stalk of the flax plant, a longer, stronger, more structured fiber that behaves differently the moment you put it on.

### Cooling: linen's home turf

If your only question is "what keeps me coolest in the heat," linen wins, and it isn't especially close. Flax fibers are hollow and highly conductive, so linen pulls heat and moisture away from the body and lets them evaporate fast. That's why a linen shirt feels crisp and airy on a sweltering day when a cotton one has gone limp and damp. Linen can absorb a good amount of moisture and still feel dry to the touch, and it dries quickly once it does get wet.

Cotton breathes well too, far better than any synthetic, and it's lovely in mild warmth. But it absorbs moisture and holds onto it, so on the hottest, stickiest afternoon, cotton clings and linen still feels like a breeze. For peak-summer dressing, sweaty commutes, and hot-sleeper bedding, linen is the fabric that keeps its cool.

### Softness and everyday comfort: cotton's edge

Cotton's advantage is how it feels from the very first wear. It's soft right off the rack, gentle on sensitive skin, and forgiving for the things you want cozy on day one: t-shirts, underwear, pajamas, baby clothes. Linen starts crisper, sometimes almost stiff, and earns its softness over months of washing and wearing. Plenty of people fall for that lived-in, rumpled linen feel, but it asks for a little patience to get there. If you want comfort with no break-in period, cotton is the gentler hello.

There's a texture difference, too. Cotton is smooth and uniform. Linen has a slight, irregular slub to it, the tiny thick-and-thin variation that gives it that relaxed, expensive look. One isn't better than the other; they're simply different moods.

> Neither one is plastic. That single fact already puts both of them ahead of most of your closet.

### Durability: linen plays the long game

Linen is one of the strongest natural fibers there is, stronger than cotton thread for thread, which is why a good linen piece can outlast cotton by years and tends to soften rather than wear thin. Antique linen sheets get handed down for a reason. Cotton is durable enough for everyday life and stands up to frequent washing, but a cheap cotton tee will thin and lose shape faster than a well-made linen shirt will show its age. If you buy a piece hoping to keep it for a decade, linen is built for that kind of patience.

### The footprint: where they grow apart

Both are plants, but they ask very different things of the land. Flax is famously low-maintenance: it grows in cool, damp climates like northern France and Belgium, usually on rainfall alone, with little irrigation and few pesticides, and nearly every part of the plant gets used. Conventional cotton is thirstier and far more chemically sprayed, one of the most pesticide-heavy crops in the world, and it can take thousands of liters of water to grow a single shirt's worth.

The gap narrows with organic cotton, which drops the synthetic pesticides and usually grows on less water, and it's the version worth reaching for when you choose cotton. But on raw footprint, linen starts from an easier place. It's one of the lightest-touch fabrics you can wear.

### The wrinkle question

Linen wrinkles. There's no getting around it, and it's the single most common reason people hesitate. The crinkle is the price of all that breathability and that loose, airy weave. The trick is to stop fighting it. Linen's rumple is part of its look, the reason it reads as relaxed and quietly luxurious rather than fussy. Cotton wrinkles less and presses crisper, so if you want a sharp, smooth finish with no thought, cotton is the easier pick. If you've made peace with a little softness in the silhouette, linen rewards you everywhere else.

### The blend trap

One real watch-out applies to both: the "linen-look" and "cotton-rich" pieces that quietly mix in polyester to cut costs. A "linen blend" can be mostly plastic, and a "cotton-rich" shirt can carry enough polyester to lose much of what made you want cotton in the first place. The front of the listing names the fiber that sounds best; the composition tag tells you how much of it is actually there. Pure linen and pure cotton both behave the way this whole comparison describes only when they're, in fact, pure.

### What it means for your closet

Reach for linen when staying cool is the job: summer dresses, warm-weather shirts, breezy trousers, and bedding for hot sleepers. Reach for cotton when softness and easy comfort matter most: everyday tees, sleepwear, anything for sensitive or baby skin, the pieces you want soft from the first wear. Buy organic cotton where you can, and don't overthink the rest, because both of these are fibers you can feel good about wearing against your skin all day.

The real decision isn't linen or cotton. It's making sure the piece in your hand is genuinely one of them, and not a synthetic stand-in wearing the name.
