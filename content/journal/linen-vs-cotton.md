This is the rare comparison where there's no villain. Linen and cotton are both natural plant fibers, both breathable, both free of the plastic that hides in most of the clothing rack. Neither will quietly shed microplastics into your laundry water. So this isn't a question of safe versus not. It's a question of which good option is right for the piece in your hand.

They come from different plants. Cotton is the fluff around a seed. Linen is spun from the stalk of the flax plant, a longer, stronger fiber that behaves differently the moment you put it on.

### Cooling: linen's home turf

If your only question is "what keeps me coolest in the heat," linen wins, and it isn't especially close. Flax fibers are hollow and highly conductive, so linen pulls heat and moisture away from the body and dries fast, which is why it feels crisp and airy when cotton has gone limp and damp. Cotton breathes well too, far better than any synthetic, but it absorbs moisture and holds it. On the hottest, stickiest day, linen is the fabric that still feels like air.

### Softness and everyday comfort: cotton's edge

Cotton's advantage is how it feels from the very first wear. It's soft right off the rack, gentle on sensitive skin, and more forgiving for things like t-shirts, underwear, and baby clothes. Linen starts crisper, almost stiff, and earns its softness over time. Many people love that lived-in linen feel, but it asks for a little patience, and a few washes, to get there.

> Neither one is plastic. That single fact already puts both of them ahead of most of your closet.

### Durability and footprint

Linen is one of the strongest natural fibers, so a good linen piece can outlast cotton by years and tends to soften rather than wear out. Flax is also a famously low-maintenance crop: it needs little irrigation and few pesticides, often growing on rain alone. Conventional cotton is thirstier and more chemically sprayed, though organic cotton closes much of that gap. Linen wrinkles more, which is the price of all that breathability, and a feature if you've made peace with the look.

### What it means for your closet

Reach for linen when staying cool is the job: summer dresses, warm-weather shirts, breezy trousers, bedding for hot sleepers. Reach for cotton when softness and easy comfort matter most: everyday tees, anything for sensitive or baby skin, the pieces you want soft on day one. Both are fibers you can feel good about wearing against your skin.
