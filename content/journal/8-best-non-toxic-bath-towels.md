faq:
  - q: "Are microfiber towels bad for you?"
    a: "Microfiber is polyester and polyamide split into extremely fine filaments, so a quick-dry towel is a plastic textile with a friendlier name. It sheds those filaments through the wash, and you are rubbing it into warm damp skin with the pores open, which is the least favorable timing in the day. Natural fiber does the same job with none of that question attached."
  - q: "What does GSM mean on a towel?"
    a: "Grams per square meter, which is fabric density. Roughly 400 to 600 GSM is a standard absorbent bath towel, 600 to 900 is the plush hotel weight, and below 400 is a lightweight or travel towel. Higher GSM means more absorbent and slower to dry, which matters more than most shoppers expect in a humid bathroom."
  - q: "Why do my towels smell after a few days?"
    a: "Because they are staying damp long enough for bacteria to establish, which fabric softener and synthetic content both make worse by coating or trapping the fibers. Linen and lyocell release moisture faster than heavy cotton terry, so they tend to stay fresher between washes. Skipping softener entirely also restores absorbency that most people assume their towels lost with age."
  - q: "Is bamboo the same as lyocell?"
    a: "Not necessarily, and the distinction is the whole thing. Conventional bamboo viscose is made by dissolving the plant in a process that does not recover its solvents. Bamboo lyocell uses a closed-loop process that captures and reuses them, which is why it scores well and generic bamboo viscose does not. If a product page says only bamboo with no process named, assume viscose."
  - q: "Should I wash new towels before using them?"
    a: "Yes, and more than once. New terry carries manufacturing finishes and often a softener applied at the mill to make it feel good in the store, both of which sit on the fiber and block absorbency. Two or three hot washes with nothing added strips it, and the towel gets more absorbent afterward."
  - q: "Is organic cotton worth it for something I am not wearing?"
    a: "A towel has more skin contact per use than most clothing, on skin that is warm and damp rather than dry. A full GOTS certificate also covers dyeing and finishing rather than only the field, which is where the chemistry that reaches you gets added. For a purchase that lasts years, it is the swap to make."

The bathroom is the room nobody audits. Closets get cleaned out, skincare gets scrutinized ingredient by ingredient, and then the towels sit there for a decade because towels feel like furniture rather than something worn.

They are worn, though, and on the worst possible terms. A bath towel touches nearly every inch of the body, twice a day, on skin that is warm, damp and freshly exposed. Nothing else in a home gets that combination of full-body contact and open pores. The category has gone synthetic anyway, because polyester dries faster and costs less to make, and quick-dry sounds like a feature rather than a description of plastic.

Microfiber is the clearest example. It is polyester and polyamide split into filaments finer than silk, sold as the technical upgrade, and it is the thing you are rubbing into wet skin. The six-for-$30 set at the big-box store is a cotton blend cut with enough polyester to hold its shape through industrial finishing. Neither one announces itself on the front of the packaging.

Real towels are still made, in organic cotton, linen and closed-loop lyocell, and they last long enough that the price per year stops looking like a splurge. Here are eight, scored:

## The short version

Organic cotton for softness, linen or waffle weave for drying speed, closed-loop lyocell if you want both. Any of the eight beats microfiber, and a good towel outlasts a cheap one by years, which is where the price difference goes.

One buying note that applies across the category: home textiles are sold by size and by piece count, so the price you see first is often the washcloth or the hand towel. Check which variant is selected before you judge the number. Then scan the towels already on your shelf with the Toxome app and start with whichever one you reach for daily.
