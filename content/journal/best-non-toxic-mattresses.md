  - q: "Why is there fiberglass in mattresses?"
    a: "Because the federal flammability rule is a performance test, not a materials rule. A mattress has to survive an open flame, and the regulation does not say how. Fiberglass passes cheaply and without chemical treatment, so it became the default barrier in budget beds. California has now banned it in mattresses sold in the state from January 1, 2027, which is why so many brands started advertising fiberglass-free in the last two years."
  - q: "Does a GOLS certified mattress mean the whole mattress is organic?"
    a: "No. GOLS certifies the latex and only the latex. It requires at least 95% certified organic natural rubber by polymer weight and caps the processing chemicals, and it says nothing about your cover, your wool, your coils, your adhesives, or your flame barrier. A bed whose only certification is GOLS is certified from the third layer down."
  - q: "What is the difference between GOTS certified cotton and a GOTS certified mattress?"
    a: "A component claim and a finished-product license are not the same thing. Most brands certify the cotton, then write about the mattress. Very few hold a license on the assembled bed. If a brand has one, it will publish the number, because the number is the whole point of having it."
  - q: "Does GREENGUARD Gold mean a mattress is non-toxic?"
    a: "It means the finished product was tested in an environmental chamber against emissions limits for hundreds of volatile organic compounds. It is an air-quality test, not a materials audit, and a polyurethane foam mattress can hold the certification. It is worth having. It is not a verdict on what the bed is made of."
  - q: "Do these mattresses have a Toxome score?"
    a: "No, and that is deliberate. The Toxome score reads fiber composition, which is the right question for something you wear and the wrong one for something built from latex, coils, and a flame barrier. Scoring the cover fabric and calling it a mattress score would tell you nothing. So these are sorted by what their certifications cover, with the scope of each one printed on the card."
  - q: "Is a mattress without any flame barrier legal?"
    a: "Yes. The rule is about passing the burn test, not about carrying a barrier. Remove enough of the fast-burning synthetics and a bed can pass on its own construction. Two of the mattresses here do exactly that and use no barrier of any kind."

On January 1, 2027, California makes it illegal to sell a mattress containing textile fiberglass. It is the only enacted ban in the country, and because no manufacturer builds a separate bed for one state, it works as a national deadline.

You have probably seen the result already. Every mattress brand now advertises that it is fiberglass-free, and few of them will tell you what they use instead.

That gap is the story. The federal flammability rule, 16 CFR Part 1633, is a performance standard: a mattress has to survive an open flame for a set time. It does not say how. Fiberglass became the industry answer because it is cheap and passes without chemical treatment, woven into a sock under a cover whose tag warns you not to remove it. You take the cover off eventually, and the fibers get into the room. California's health department puts remediation at thousands of dollars.

So *fiberglass-free* tells you what a brand took out, and nothing about what it put in. Ask what the barrier is instead. Two answers hold up: organic wool, or no barrier at all, which a manufacturer earns by removing enough synthetics that the bed passes the burn test on its own construction.

Then read the certifications by what they cover. GOLS certifies latex and stops there, which leaves the cover, the wool, the coils, the glue and the barrier outside the certificate. A bed sold as a GOLS certified organic mattress is certified from the third layer down.

None of these mattresses carry a Toxome score, and that is on purpose. The score reads fiber composition, the right question for a shirt and the wrong one for a bed built from latex and steel. Scoring the cover fabric and calling that number a mattress score would tell you nothing. So each card below prints the certifications, and under them, the object each certification attaches to.

Eight mattresses that will tell you what is inside them, ordered by how much their certifications cover. Prices are for a queen, checked on July 21, 2026.

## Where a certification stops

The mattresses that did not make this list carry real certifications pointed at something smaller than the bed. That is a harder thing to spot, so here is what it looks like.

**A certification on the factory.** One brand's page states that it holds a GOLS certified facility, able to manufacture organic finished goods. Read it twice. A certified factory capable of making organic products is not a statement that the product in your cart is one. The certificate is real. The object it describes is a building.

**A certification on the glue.** The same page advertises a GREENGUARD Gold certified adhesive. The adhesive is a rounding error by weight in a mattress. At a glance the badge reads as though the bed was tested. It was not.

**A certification on one layer.** The most common version, and the most reasonable-looking. GOLS on the latex, then paragraphs about an organic mattress. The cover, the wool, the coils, and the barrier are all outside the certificate. It is a true statement about the third layer down, printed at a size that suggests it covers everything.

**A barrier described but never named.** Several beds in the wider market disclose a dedicated fire-barrier layer, confirm it is fiberglass-free, and never say what it is. One describes its barrier as made from natural or silica-based materials, which is the phrasing the industry uses for the fiberglass family. If a brand will not name the barrier, treat that as the answer.

The test that cuts through all four: for every badge on the page, ask what object it attaches to. Then ask whether that object is the mattress. If the brand publishes a certificate number, it is telling you it expects the question. Two brands on this list do.
