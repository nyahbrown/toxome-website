Two t-shirts hang on the same rack, same white, same price. One tag says cotton, the other says polyester. Under the store's air conditioning they feel almost identical. Then you wear them on a warm day, and your body tells you which is which.

Cotton is a plant. It grows in a field, gets picked, and is spun into thread. Polyester is plastic. It begins as crude oil, gets cooked into a polymer, and is drawn into a filament so fine you'd never guess it shares a family tree with a water bottle. Both end up as a soft white shirt. What they do once they're on you is where they part ways.

### Breathability: the test your skin runs all day

Cotton is built like a bundle of tiny straws. It pulls moisture off your skin and lets air pass through, which is why a cotton shirt feels cool, and why it turns heavy and damp once you really sweat. Polyester does close to the opposite. It repels water by nature, so instead of absorbing sweat it traps heat against you. That held warmth is a gift in a winter base layer and a punishment in July. It's also why polyester is the fabric most likely to hold onto smell: the bacteria behind body odor thrive in the warm, damp pocket it creates.

### What polyester sheds

Because polyester is plastic, it doesn't simply sit there. Every wash sheds microscopic plastic fibers. Researchers at the University of Plymouth found a single household load can release hundreds of thousands of these microfibers, which slip through water treatment and settle in rivers, oceans, and eventually the food chain. The fabric sheds onto you too, dry, through ordinary wear. Cotton sheds as well, but a cotton fiber is cellulose, the same material as a leaf. It breaks down. A polyester fiber is plastic, and plastic stays.

> Cotton comes from a field and goes back to one. Polyester comes from an oil well and never really leaves.

### Where cotton isn't innocent

This isn't a clean sweep. Conventional cotton is one of the thirstiest, most heavily sprayed crops on the planet. By some estimates a single shirt's worth can take more than 2,000 liters of water to grow, and the crop absorbs an outsized share of the world's insecticides. That is the case for organic cotton, which yields the same fiber without the synthetic pesticides and usually on far less water. So the honest framing isn't "cotton good, polyester bad." Cotton's worst problems happen out in the field, before the shirt exists. Polyester's problems are stitched into the shirt and follow it onto your skin and into the water for the length of its very long life.

### What it means for your closet

For anything worn against the skin all day, a tee, underwear, a base layer, anything you sleep in, natural fibers win on comfort and on what they leave behind. Reach for cotton, and organic cotton when you can find it, over polyester and "poly blends." Polyester earns its place in a rain shell or a piece of gym kit you want to dry fast. The trouble is how quietly it has crept into everything else.
